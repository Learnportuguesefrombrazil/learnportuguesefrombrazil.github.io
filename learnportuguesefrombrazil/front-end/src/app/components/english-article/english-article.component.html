Study Suggests Prescribing Fruits and Vegetables for Diabetes

We all know that fruits and vegetables are good for us, but are we eating enough of them?

Research has shown that only 1 in 10 Americans eats enough fruits and vegetables. Often this is because healthy food is more expensive, or because people live in poor neighborhoods where fresh food is not easy to find.

Poor diet has also been shown to be a cause of Type 2 diabetes, which can lead to other health problems such as high blood pressure, heart disease and an increased risk of stroke.

Now a new study suggests that prescribing cheap or free fruits and vegetables to Americans with diabetes could prevent 292,000 serious health events such as heart disease or stroke, as well as saving $39.6 billion in health care costs over 25 years.

The researchers created a simulation model using data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey. The survey included 6.5 million Americans aged between 40 and 79 who had diabetes and did not have enough access to healthy food.

To estimate the effect of a food prescription program on these people, they also used data from previous studies of 20 food prescription programs for people with similar health problems who could not easily get healthy food.

The study found that a program providing free or discounted fruits and vegetables over a 25-year period would cost almost $44.3 billion. However, on top of the $39.6 billion saved in health care costs, almost $4.8 billion would also be saved in lost productivity at workplaces around the country.

The study also found that the program would add a total of 260,000 years of perfect health to the lives of the participants as a whole.

Although food prescription programs have been shown to have clear health benefits, the long-term effects of a national US program had not previously been studied.
