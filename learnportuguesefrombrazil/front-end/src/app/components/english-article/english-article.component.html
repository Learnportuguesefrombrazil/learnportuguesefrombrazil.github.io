Survey: Well-being Worsens, but Employers Don't Notice

Do you ever think your boss doesn't understand how you feel? If so, you're far from alone, according to a survey of well-being in the workplace.

Most employees feel their well-being at work has worsened or stayed the same since 2022, the survey by Workplace Intelligence and Deloitte found.

It said many employees are "struggling with unacceptably low levels of well-being." But most senior managers actually think their employees' well-being is getting better.

The survey included 3,150 senior managers, junior managers and employees in the US, Canada, the UK and Australia.

Only 36% of employees said their physical well-being has improved, while 33% said their mental well-being has improved.

But among the senior managers, 80% said they believed their employees' physical well-being is better in 2023, and 77% said their employees' mental well-being has improved.

Deloitte said well-being at work has worsened for most people since the coronavirus pandemic. And it said managers are struggling with their own poor well-being at work.

Three-quarters of senior managers, 64% of junior managers and 60% of employees said they are thinking about leaving their job for one that would "better support their well-being."

However, this is nothing new. A survey by Adecco in 2022 also found that 45% of employees thought their employers did not offer enough support for their well-being.

Deloitte said 85% of managers plan to do more for their employees' well-being over the next two years.

But 70% of managers said problems such as company policies, too much work, and an "unsupportive workplace culture" make it hard for them to support employees' well-being.
